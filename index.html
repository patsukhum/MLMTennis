Background & Motivation

Background

  • Professional tennis players compete in tournaments throughout the year. Depending on how well they perform, they earn a certain number of ranking points
  • Tennis players typically rise in the rankings age as they age until they reach their peak. They subsequently fall as they get older and past their physical prime

Motivation

  • We're interested in the relationship between a player's age and tennis rank because we're tennis fans!
  • From a player's perspective, this information could also be useful...If I am ranked 1,000 at age 18, how far can I expect to climb?
  • From an investment perspective, our inquiry is also crucial. Tennis players typically don't make much money until they're ranked above 200...If you never expect to be ranked that high, it may be time to hang up your racquet!

Research Questions

RQ 1: How do a player’s age and home continent predict ranking growth?

Why would a player’s continent matter? Tennis’s popularity varies significantly by country and, by extension, by continent. If tennis is popular in a given region, this usually means there is an institutional body (such as the USTA in the United States) that provides player development, support, and financing. A player from a country and continent where tennis is popular is also more likely to have support from fans.

RQ 2: How do a player’s age and average match performance – e.g. the proportion of serve points won per match, the proportion of breakpoints saved per match–predict his ranking growth?

This question is perhaps more intuitive. We would expect a player with better average match performance statistics to ascend to higher rankings. But what average match statistics are particularly important, and how much difference in ranking, after considering age, do they predict?

Key Takeaways

  • Match statistics – the proportion of first serves won, the proportion of serves won, and the proportion of break points saved – predict differences in a player’s rank. The differences only shift the curves up/down however and do not affect the slope/curvature.
  • Unexpectedly, playing hand is not statistically significant in our models.
  • Again, there’s still a lot of variation not captured by the fixed effects.
